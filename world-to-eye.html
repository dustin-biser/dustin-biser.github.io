World Space to Eye Space Transformation

Here we explain and derive the transformations responsible for transforming vectors and points from our World Coordinate System $WCS$ to our Eye Coordinate System $ECS$ (also know as the Camera Coordinate System). The $ECS$-to-$WCS$ transformation is a rather integral part of the full graphics transformation process, which in total is responsible for mapping vectors and points from object space all the way to screen space.

The conversion from $WCS$ to $ECS$ can be accomplished by applying two transformations in the following order:

  1. Translating by the distance between the two spaces so their origins coincide.
  2. Applying a Change of Basis from one coordinate system to the other.

Note that steps 1 and 2 are needed for transforming points from $WCS$ to $ECS$, but to transform vectors, all that is needed is the Change of Basis step. This comes form the fact that vectors have direction and magnitude, but no position.

The translation step will involve moving all points given with respect to the $WCS$ a distance and direction equal to moving the $ECS$ origin so it coincides with the $WCS$ origin. The location of the $ECS$ origin can be described in $WCS$ components to give $[\vec{E}]_{WCS} = [E_x,E_y,E_z]^T$. Using the components of this vector, we can construct a translation matrix in homogeneous form that will move our points to the desired location. That matrix is the following: \[ \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & -E_x \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -E_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -E_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right]\]

Once the origin of $WCS$ coincides with the origin of $ECS$, all that is left is to apply a series of rotations so that their unit axes line up in a desired way. An efficient way of applying such rotations without the need for evaluating expensive trigonometric functions is to construct a Change of Basis Transformation Matrix. Such a matrix allows one to transform vectors given in one basis, to the components given in another basis (the same holds for points, so long as the origin of each space coincides, which was the reason for first applying a translation).

Assume that $ECS$ is composed of the basis vectors $\{\hat{x_e}, \hat{y_e}, \hat{z_e}\}$, which make up its unit axes. It is typical in graphics to describe the unit vectors of $ECS$ using world coordinates. This is analogous to describing the orientation of our viewing-camera with respect to the orientation of world space. Each basis vector of $ECS$ can be decomposed into its own combination of $WCS$ components given as follows: \begin{eqnarray*} [\hat{x_e}]_{WCS} & = [a_x, a_y, a_z]^T \\ [\hat{y_e}]_{WCS} & = [b_x, b_y, b_z]^T \\ [\hat{z_e}]_{WCS} & = [c_x, c_y, c_z]^T \end{eqnarray*}

Since the unit vectors of $WCS$ and $ECS$ separately form a basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$, there exists a unique matrix $\underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P}$ such that given any vector $\vec{u} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, \begin{equation*} [\vec{u}]_{WCS} = \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P} [\vec{u}]_{ECS} \end{equation*}

Here, the matrix $P$ is called the Change of Basis Transformation Matrix from $ECS$ to $WCS$, because it allows one to convert the components of $\vec{u}$ from eye-coordinates to world-coordinates. The columns of $P$ are the unit vectors of $ECS$ given in world space coordinates. \begin{equation*} \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} [\hat{x_e}]_{WCS} & [\hat{y_e}]_{WCS} & [\hat{z_e}]_{WCS} \end{array} \right] \end{equation*} This stems from the fact that our change of basis matrix $P$ represents a linear transformation. Such a matrix can be constructed by settings its columns to the transformed basis vectors of the domain space.

Unfortunately, our goal is to convert vector components the other way around, that is from $WCS$ to $ECS$. This is not too big of a problem though, as we shall soon see.
