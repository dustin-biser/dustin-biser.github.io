World Space to Eye Space Transformation

Here we explain and derive the transformations responsible for transforming vectors and points from our World Coordinate System $WCS$ to our Eye Coordinate System $ECS$ (also know as the Camera Coordinate System). The $ECS$-to-$WCS$ transformation is a rather integral part of the full graphics transformation process, which in total is responsible for mapping vectors and points from object space all the way to screen space.

The conversion from $WCS$ to $ECS$ can be accomplished by applying two transformations in the following order:

  1. Translating by the distance between the two spaces so their origins coincide.
  2. Applying a Change of Basis from one coordinate system to the other.

Note that steps 1 and 2 are needed for transforming points from $WCS$ to $ECS$, but to transform vectors, all that is needed is the Change of Basis step. This comes form the fact that vectors have direction and magnitude, but no position.

The translation step will involve moving all points a distance and direction equal to moving the $ECS$ origin so it coincides with the $WCS$ origin. The location of the eye-space origin can be described in world-space components to give $[\boldsymbol{\vec{E}}]_{WCS} = [E_x,E_y,E_z]^T$. Using the components of this vector, we can construct a translation matrix in homogeneous form that will move our points to the desired location. That matrix is the following: \begin{equation} \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & -E_x \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -E_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -E_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \end{equation}

Once the origin of $WCS$ coincides with the origin of $ECS$, all that is left is to apply a series of rotations so that their unit axes line up in a desired way. An efficient way of applying such rotations without the need for evaluating expensive trigonometric functions is to construct a Change of Basis Transformation Matrix. Such a matrix allows one to transform vectors given in one basis, to the components given in another basis (the same holds for points, so long as the origin of each space coincides, which was the reason for first applying a translation).

Assume that $ECS$ is composed of the basis vectors $\{ \boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}} \}$, which make up its unit axes. It is typical in graphics to describe the unit vectors of eye-space using world-space components. This is analogous to describing the orientation of our viewing-camera with respect to the orientation of world space. Each basis vector of $ECS$ can be decomposed into its own combination of $WCS$ components given as follows: \begin{equation} [\boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}]_{WCS} = [a_x, a_y, a_z]^T, \; [\boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}]_{WCS} = [b_x, b_y, b_z]^T, \; [\boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}}]_{WCS} = [c_x, c_y, c_z]^T \end{equation}

Since the unit vectors of $WCS$ and $ECS$ separately form a basis of $\mathbb{R}^3$, there exists a unique matrix $\underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P}$ such that given any vector $\boldsymbol{\vec{u}} \in \mathbb{R}^3$, \begin{equation} [\boldsymbol{\vec{u}}]_{WCS} = \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P} [\boldsymbol{\vec{u}}]_{ECS} \end{equation}

Here, the matrix $P$ is called the Change of Basis Transformation Matrix from $ECS$ to $WCS$, because it allows one to convert the components of $\boldsymbol{\vec{u}}$ from eye-coordinates to world-coordinates. The columns of $P$ are the unit vectors of $ECS$ given in world space coordinates. \begin{equation} \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} [\boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}]_{WCS} & [\boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}]_{WCS} & [\boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}}]_{WCS} \end{array} \right] \end{equation} This stems from the fact that our change of basis matrix $P$ represents a linear transformation, and a matrix that corresponds to a linear transformation can be constructed by settings its columns equal to the transformed basis vectors of the domain coordinate space.

Unfortunately, our goal is to convert vector components the other way around, that is from $WCS$ to $ECS$. This is not too big of a problem though, as we soon shall see.

Notice first what happens if $P^{-1}$ exists. We can multiply on the left of $(3)$ by the inverse $P^{-1}$ in order to arrive at \begin{equation} \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P^{-1}} [\boldsymbol{\vec{u}}]_{WCS} = [\boldsymbol{\vec{u}}]_{ECS} \end{equation}

which says that the inverse $P^{-1}$ of our change of basis matrix takes vectors with components given in world-coordinates and converts them to eye-coordinates. This is precisely the coordinate transformation we desire.

Let us take a moment now to show that $P^{-1}$ actually exists. We do this by showing that $P$ is an orthogonal matrix, whose columns are orthonormal, and thus linearly independent. To prove that $P$ is an orthogonal matrix, all that is required is for $P^T P = I$ to hold. For simplicity of notation, we define the following unit vectors $\boldsymbol{\hat{x}} = [\boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}]_{WCS}^T, \;\; \boldsymbol{\hat{y}} = [\boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}]_{WCS}^T, \;\; \boldsymbol{\hat{z}} = [\boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}}]_{WCS}^T$ that represent the world-space components of our eye-space basis vectors. Using these vectors we can compute $P^T P$ as follows: \begin{eqnarray*} P^T P & = & \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\hat{x}}^T \\ \boldsymbol{\hat{y}}^T \\ \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}^T \end{bmatrix} \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\hat{x}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{y}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{z}} \end{bmatrix} \\ \\ & = & \begin{bmatrix} \boldsymbol{\hat{x}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{x}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{x}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{y}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{x}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{z}} & \\ \boldsymbol{\hat{y}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{x}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{y}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{y}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{y}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{z}} & \\ \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{x}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{y}} & \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}^T \boldsymbol{\hat{z}} & \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{eqnarray*} Since $WCS$ and $ECS$ both stem from orthonormal bases, the change of basis from one to the other preserves length and angles. This comes from the fact that if two vectors are orthogonal with respect to one coordinate space, they must be orthgonal with respect to the second space, in addition to the fact that the length of a vector is the same in both coordinate spaces. The basis vectors $\boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}}$ of eye-space are orthonormal, thus after being converted to their world-space counterparts $\boldsymbol{\hat{x}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{y}}, \boldsymbol{\hat{z}}$, their orthonormality remains. This allows us to conclude that the diagonal entries of $P^T P$ must all be one, and the off diagonal entries zero, meaning \begin{equation} P^T P = I \end{equation} Therefore, $P$ is an orthogonal matrix, and $P^{-1}$ exists. This means that $(5)$ is valid and $P^{-1}$ is the change of basis matrix that transforms world-space components to eye-space components. Combining this with the property $P^{-1} = P^T$ we have \begin{equation} \underset{WCS \leftarrow ECS}{P^T} = \underset{ECS \leftarrow WCS}{P} \end{equation} Taking the transpose of $(4)$ gives us an explicit form for $P^T$ \begin{eqnarray} \underset{ECS \leftarrow WCS}{P} & = & \begin{bmatrix} [\boldsymbol{\hat{x_e}}]^T_{WCS} \\ [\boldsymbol{\hat{y_e}}]^T_{WCS} \\ [\boldsymbol{\hat{z_e}}]^T_{WCS} \\ \end{bmatrix} & = & \begin{bmatrix} a_x & a_y & a_z \\ b_x & b_y & b_z \\ c_x & c_y & c_z \\ \end{bmatrix} \\ \end{eqnarray}

We are almost finished. All that is left in order to construct the full $WCS$ to $ECS$ transformation matrix is to convert $(8)$ to homogeneous form, then multiply it on the right by our translation matrix from $(1)$. The final result is given below: \begin{equation} \underset{ECS \leftarrow WCS}{M} = \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} a_x & a_y & a_z & 0 \\ b_x & b_y & b_z & 0 \\ c_x & c_y & c_z & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \cdot \left[ \begin{array}{ccc} 1 & 0 & 0 & -E_x \\ 0 & 1 & 0 & -E_y \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & -E_z \\ 0 & 0 & 0 & 1 \end{array} \right] \end{equation} Multiplying points or vectors on the left by the matrix $M$ will transform their components from world-space to eye-space.
